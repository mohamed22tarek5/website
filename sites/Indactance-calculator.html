Theory of Inductors in Series and Parallel

Inductors in Series

When inductors are connected in series, the equivalent inductance is the sum of the individual inductances:

Leq = L1 + L2 + L3 + ... + Ln

The equivalent inductance is always larger than the largest individual inductor in the series.

Inductors in Parallel

When inductors are connected in parallel, the equivalent inductance is calculated using:

1/Leq = 1/L1 + 1/L2 + 1/L3 + ... + 1/Ln

The equivalent inductance is always smaller than the smallest individual inductor in the parallel connection.

Physical Explanation

Series connection: The same current flows through each inductor, and the voltage drops add up. The total inductance is the sum because the magnetic fields add constructively.

Parallel connection: The voltage across each inductor is the same, but the currents divide. The total inductance is less than any individual inductance because the magnetic fields interact.

Important Notes

  • These formulas assume no mutual coupling between inductors
  • For mutually coupled inductors, calculations are more complex
  • In real circuits, always consider the inductor's DC resistance and self-resonant frequency

  • 1 millihenry (mH) = 1,000 microhenries (uH)

  • 1 nanohenry (nH) = 1,000 picohenries (pH)
  • 1 kilohenry (kH) = 1,000 Henry (H)

Air-Core Inductor Formula

The inductance of an air-core solenoid is calculated using:

L = (mu_0 * N^2 * A) / l

Where:

  • L = Inductance in Henries
  • mu_0 = Permeability of free space (4pi x 10^-7 H/m)
  • N = Number of turns
  • A = Cross-sectional area of coil (m^2)
  • l = Length of coil (m)
