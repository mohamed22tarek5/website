Theory of Resistors in Series and Parallel

Resistors in Series

When resistors are connected in series, the equivalent resistance is the sum of all individual resistances:

Req = R1 + R2 + R3 + ... + Rn

This means the equivalent resistance is always greater than the largest individual resistor in the series.

Resistors in Parallel

When resistors are connected in parallel, the equivalent resistance is calculated using:

1/Req = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ... + 1/Rn

For just two resistors in parallel, this simplifies to:

Req = (R1 × R2) / (R1 + R2)

This means the equivalent resistance is always smaller than the smallest individual resistor in the parallel connection.

Physical Explanation

Series connection: In series, the same current flows through each resistor, but the voltage is divided across all resistors. Each resistor adds impedance to the flow of current, increasing the total resistance.

Parallel connection: In parallel, each resistor has the same voltage across it, but the current is divided among all paths. This creates multiple paths for current to flow, decreasing the total resistance.

Ohm's Law

Remember that resistors follow Ohm's Law:

V = I × R

Where:

  • V is the voltage across the resistor (in volts, V)
  • I is the current through the resistor (in amperes, A)
  • R is the resistance (in ohms, Ω)
